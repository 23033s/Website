Plastic Pollution Impacts | Understanding the Consequences


Plastic pollution is not just an environmental issue, it also directly affects marine life, our food chain, and even human health.


Key Impacts of Plastic Pollution

  • Harm to Marine Life: Animals can ingest plastic or get entangled, leading to injury or death.
  • Food Chain Contamination: Microplastics enter fish and seafood, which humans then consume, risking health problems.
  • Environmental Damage: Plastic pollution disrupts ecosystems and habitats, affecting biodiversity.
  • Human Health Risks: Chemicals from plastics can leach into water and food, potentially causing illnesses.
  • Economic Costs: Cleaning up plastic pollution is expensive and harms industries like tourism and fishing.

Harm to Marine Life:

Every year, millions of marine animals are harmed by plastic waste. Here are some examples:


Sea turtles often mistake plastic bags for jellyfish and eat them, leading to internal blockages and a slow suffocating death.


Birds like albatrosses feed plastic pieces to their chicks, thinking it's food.


This causes malnutrition which is a condition caused by a poor diet, where a person or animal doesn't get enough of the right nutrients such as vitamins and minerals which can be fatal.


Seals and dolphins also often get trapped in old fishing nets or six-pack rings, which can lead to injury, infection, or drowning.


These incidents are so common and deeply damaging to marine ecosystems.


Food Chain Contamination:

Tiny plastic particles called microplastics are often eaten by small sea creatures like plankton. These are then eaten by fish, which are eaten by larger animals, then eventually by humans. This means the plastic pollution that begins in the ocean can make its way onto our dinner plates. Scientists have found microplastics in seafood, salt, and even drinking water. This raises serious concerns about how plastic is entering our bodies through the food we eat.


Environmental Damage:

Plastic doesn’t just go away, it takes hundreds of years to break down. As it accumulates, it covers beaches, pollutes rivers, and piles up in the ocean, forming massive floating garbage patches like the Great Pacific Garbage Patch. This disrupts natural habitats, blocks sunlight from reaching underwater plants, and threatens the survival of both plants and animals. Coral reefs, mangroves, and other ecosystems are especially sensitive and can be badly damaged by plastic buildup.


Human Health Risks:

Some plastics contain harmful chemicals such as BPA (bisphenol A) and phthalates, which are also found in personal care products. These substances are known to disrupt hormones and have been linked to a range of health issues. BPA, for example, is associated with hormone imbalances, an increased risk of cancer, and obesity. Phthalates may reduce testosterone levels, impact fertility, and interfere with healthy child development. When plastic containers are heated or left in the sun, these chemicals can leak into our food and drinks. Over time, this can lead to serious health issues. Tiny plastic pieces, called microplastics, have also been found in our food and water. Scientists are still studying their full effects, but early research shows they may be dangerous to our bodies.


Economic Costs:

Governments and communities spend billions of dollars each year cleaning up plastic waste from streets, rivers, and coastlines. Cleaning up plastic waste can be quite costly, especially in developing countries, where waste management systems may be limited so plastic pollution can overwhelm communities. Plastic pollution also affects industries like tourism, no one wants to visit a beach full of rubbish. Fishing is also affected as plastic harms fish stocks and damages equipment.


Kaitiakitanga Quote: "Manaaki whenua, manaaki tāngata, haere whakamua" - This whakataukī translates to "If we take care of the land and take care of the people, we will take care of the future."


This quote signifies a deep, inseparable connection (physical and spiritual) between the Māori people and the land.


The quote is more than just words; it represents a worldview where the land is not simply a resource, but also an essential part of identity, heritage, and wellbeing.


See the land as yourself and protect it, protect it like you would protect yourself.
